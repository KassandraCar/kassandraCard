## Overview

WashPIRG (Washington Public Interest Research Group) is a student-led advocacy organization that runs campaigns focused on environmental sustainability, public health, and civic engagement. As Fundraising Coordinator for the University of Washington chapter, I led student-driven fundraising initiatives that provided the financial infrastructure for our advocacy campaigns. This role pushed me to develop skills I did not expect to find meaningful: communication under pressure, systems thinking in a mission-driven context, and the discipline of translating values into measurable action.

---

## Context

Advocacy organizations depend on resources to function. A campaign with a compelling message and a committed team still stalls without sustainable funding. At WashPIRG, fundraising was not a peripheral function. It was foundational to everything else we did.

WashPIRG campaigns at UW addressed issues including environmental protection, affordable healthcare, consumer rights, and civic participation. These are causes that matter to students but require more than awareness. They require organized, funded effort. My role as Fundraising Coordinator meant I was responsible for making sure that effort was possible.

I joined WashPIRG because I cared about public interest advocacy, and I took on the fundraising coordinator role because I wanted to understand how organizations sustain their work long-term, not just during moments of urgency.

---

## My Role

As Fundraising Coordinator, I was responsible for planning and managing the chapter's fundraising operations. This included:

- Organizing and managing campus fundraising drives
- Coordinating student volunteers for outreach and donor engagement
- Communicating campaign impact to prospective and current donors
- Setting funding goals and tracking progress against them
- Ensuring transparency and accountability in financial reporting
- Translating complex policy goals into clear, compelling narratives for student audiences

This meant working simultaneously as a planner, a communicator, a team manager, and an advocate.

---

## Skills Developed

The Fundraising Coordinator role strengthened my ability to:

- Communicate persuasively and ethically, without misrepresenting the work
- Set measurable goals and track performance against them
- Manage volunteers under time constraints and competing priorities
- Handle rejection and remain resilient across sustained campaigns
- Align mission-driven work with operational and financial structure
- Build momentum and sustain engagement over a semester-long timeline

Fundraising is often invisible labor, but it is foundational. Without sustainable funding, even the most well-intentioned campaigns cannot move forward.

---

## Impact

The fundraising work I coordinated directly supported WashPIRG campaigns that affected students across the UW campus. By maintaining consistent funding infrastructure, the chapter was able to:

- Run multi-week advocacy campaigns on environmental and consumer issues
- Support students in civic engagement efforts including voter registration and legislative outreach
- Sustain organizational capacity between campaign cycles

The impact of fundraising is often indirect, but it is real. The campaigns that reached students, the events that brought advocates together, and the organizing that led to policy conversations were all made possible in part by the financial foundation that fundraising built.

---

## Reflection

This experience reshaped how I think about infrastructure. Whether in advocacy, nonprofits, or technology, long-term impact depends on sustainable systems. Fundraising is a form of systems design. It requires understanding people, incentives, communication, and resource allocation, and all of those skills transfer directly into engineering and organizational leadership.

At WashPIRG, I also learned something about myself: I am motivated by understanding how things work structurally, not just what they accomplish on the surface. Why does this campaign need money? What happens if it runs out? Who is responsible for sustaining it? Those questions kept me engaged long after the initial excitement of joining had faded. That structural curiosity is something I bring into every project and leadership role I take on.

## Competency Reflections

### Communication

Communication is the ability to convey information clearly, accurately, and in ways that are appropriate for your audience. As Fundraising Coordinator, I communicated constantly: with donors, with volunteers, with campus organizations, and with WashPIRG leadership. Each audience required a different approach.

Communicating with prospective donors meant translating complex policy issues into personal, concrete narratives that connected to students' lives. Communicating with volunteers meant being clear about expectations, timelines, and what success looked like. Communicating progress to organizational leadership meant tracking data and presenting it honestly, including when we fell short of goals.

I learned that fundraising communication is persuasion in service of a mission, and that means holding yourself to a high standard of accuracy and integrity. Overpromising or exaggerating impact damages trust in ways that are very difficult to repair. Going forward, I will carry this standard into all professional communication, treating clarity and honesty as non-negotiable regardless of the stakes.

---

### Strategic Planning

Strategic Planning requires designing processes with a clear destination in mind and the flexibility to adapt when circumstances change. Fundraising campaigns do not run on inspiration alone. They require timelines, goal-setting, contingency plans, and ongoing evaluation of what is working.

At WashPIRG, I learned to set realistic funding targets, build outreach calendars, and adjust tactics mid-campaign when initial approaches were not producing results. I also learned to plan for volunteer attrition, which is a real and common challenge in student organizations. The experience taught me that good planning includes planning for what goes wrong.

In future project work, whether technical or organizational, I will apply this lesson by building in checkpoints, contingency plans, and honest evaluation criteria from the start rather than assuming the initial plan will hold.

---

### Personal Behavior

Personal Behavior, as a competency, refers to acting in alignment with your values, especially when it would be easier not to. Fundraising is a context where the temptation to overstate impact or use manipulative tactics is real, because the stakes feel high and the pressure to meet goals is constant. Maintaining ethical communication standards in that environment required a conscious choice.

I chose to be transparent with donors about what their money would and would not accomplish. I chose to be honest with volunteers about the difficulty of the work. I chose to report accurate progress to leadership even when the numbers were discouraging. Those choices were not always easy, but they were consistent with how I want to lead.

I learned that personal behavior is most visible under pressure. How you act when things are not going well says more about your values than how you act when everything is easy. Going forward, I will continue to treat integrity as a non-negotiable standard, particularly in leadership roles where the people around me take cues from how I behave.
