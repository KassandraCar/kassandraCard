## Why I decided to revisit my portfolio

My personal website is more than just a collection of projects — it’s a representation of who I am as a computer scientist. It showcases my work in distributed systems, embedded systems, accessibility, and community leadership.  

But after taking a closer look at it through an accessibility lens, I realized something important: I had unintentionally designed it with assumptions about who my audience was.

Like many developers, I had optimized for aesthetics and performance. I hadn’t fully considered how someone using a screen reader, keyboard-only navigation, or high-contrast settings would experience it.

This redesign was an opportunity to correct that.

---

## What I tested

To evaluate my site, I used:

- Keyboard-only navigation (tab + shift+tab)
- VoiceOver (macOS screen reader)
- Chrome Lighthouse Accessibility Audit

I focused on the following tasks:

1. Navigating the homepage using only the keyboard  
2. Reading blog content using a screen reader  
3. Accessing project links and external navigation  

---

## Problems I discovered

### 1. Missing or weak alt text (Perceivable)

Some images relied on generic or auto-generated alt text. While the site looked visually polished, the descriptive experience for screen reader users was incomplete.

For example, project thumbnails and avatar images did not meaningfully describe their purpose.

---

### 2. Inconsistent heading structure (Understandable)

Certain pages skipped heading levels or relied heavily on styled text components rather than semantic heading tags.  

This made it harder for screen reader users to navigate by heading structure.

---

### 3. Limited visible focus indicators (Operable)

While keyboard navigation technically worked, focus states were subtle and easy to miss.  

This creates friction for users who rely on visual focus cues to understand where they are on the page.

---

## What I changed

### Improved alt text

I added meaningful alt text to:
- Profile image
- Project thumbnails
- Blog images

Instead of generic labels, I now describe the purpose of the image in context.

---

### Fixed heading hierarchy

I reviewed heading usage across pages to ensure:

- One clear `<h1>` per page  
- Logical progression (`h2 → h3 → h4`)  
- No skipped structural levels  

This improves both usability and SEO.

---

### Strengthened focus visibility

I adjusted focus styles to ensure:

- Clear visual indicators  
- Sufficient contrast  
- Consistent focus behavior across interactive elements  

This significantly improved keyboard navigation clarity.

---

## Reflection

Accessibility is not a feature — it is a baseline expectation.  

By overlooking accessibility in my initial portfolio design, I unintentionally reinforced the assumption that my audience would be non-disabled. In technology spaces, that assumption contributes to ableist design norms that exclude users from professional, academic, and social opportunities.

Revisiting my own work reminded me that inclusive design is not something we “add later.” It must be intentional from the beginning.

This redesign was a small but meaningful step toward aligning my technical work with principles of accessibility and disability justice.
