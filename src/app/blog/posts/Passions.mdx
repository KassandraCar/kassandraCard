## Overview

My research sits at the intersection of computer science, neuroscience, and systems engineering. This post describes the areas I have worked in, the questions that drive my curiosity, and what I have learned about myself as a researcher and a technical thinker. Research has been central to my development at UW, both as a technical skill and as a mode of leadership.

---

## Context

I came to research through a combination of academic coursework and a growing interest in understanding how complex systems work at a fundamental level. In my early years at UW, I was drawn to computer science but felt something was missing. I wanted to understand not just how to build systems, but how the most sophisticated system we know of, the brain, actually functions and how we might model it computationally.

That curiosity led me toward neuroscience and computational biology. I began exploring how concepts from distributed systems and signal processing could be applied to understanding neural behavior. At the same time, my work at Seattle Children's Hospital and with embedded systems brought these interests into direct contact with real-world biomedical applications.

---

## My Role

Across my research work, I have taken on multiple roles depending on the context:

- As a **Research and Development Engineer at Seattle Children's Hospital**, I prototype otoacoustic emission (OAE) systems, analyze failure modes, and propose probe geometry and connector architecture improvements to increase signal fidelity.
- In **coursework and independent study**, I have modeled neural systems using the Hodgkin-Huxley framework, implemented distributed consensus protocols, and simulated biomedical sensing scenarios.
- As an **Embedded Systems Engineer intern at Lyers Lab**, I designed low-power circuits, programmed microcontrollers in C and C++, and supported hardware bring-up for constrained sensing prototypes.

Each of these roles required me to connect theoretical understanding to applied engineering decisions.

## Areas of Research

### Neural Modeling and Computational Neuroscience

Through coursework and independent exploration, I have worked with mathematical models such as the Hodgkin-Huxley model to understand how neurons generate action potentials and transmit signals. This work involves:

- Modeling differential equations that govern ion channel dynamics
- Simulating membrane potentials under varying current injections
- Exploring how parameter changes affect firing behavior
- Connecting biological interpretation with computational implementation

This research strengthened my ability to translate biological systems into mathematical abstractions, and vice versa.

### Distributed Systems and Replicated State Machines

In distributed systems work, I implemented protocols including:

- Primary-backup replication
- Paxos and Multi-Paxos
- Sharded key-value stores
- Fault-tolerant log replication

These projects required reasoning about consensus under network partitions, linearizability and safety guarantees, leader election, and garbage collection in replicated logs. Studying distributed systems deepened my appreciation for fault tolerance, both in machines and in biological systems.

### Embedded and Biomedical Systems

My embedded systems work includes designing low-power circuits, programming microcontrollers, supporting signal acquisition, and exploring biomedical sensing applications. In biomedical contexts, constraints matter significantly. Power, signal integrity, and environmental noise all influence system behavior. Designing under these constraints has shaped how I approach engineering problems more broadly.

## Impact

My research experience has shaped both the technical work I am capable of and the kind of engineer I am becoming. Working at Seattle Children's Hospital has given me direct exposure to how engineering decisions affect healthcare outcomes. My distributed systems coursework at UW, particularly CSE 452, pushed me to reason carefully about correctness under failure in a way that has transferred to how I approach other complex systems.

The longer-term impact I am working toward is research that bridges computational theory and biological systems, building models and tools that deepen our understanding of intelligence, both artificial and biological.

## Reflection

Research has taught me that the most interesting problems live at the edges of disciplines. The questions I find most compelling are rarely answerable from within a single field. Understanding how neural populations encode information requires both biology and mathematics. Designing reliable biomedical devices requires both hardware and software thinking. Working across these boundaries has been uncomfortable at times and deeply rewarding overall.

I have also learned that research requires a specific kind of patience: the patience to sit with uncertainty, to run experiments that do not confirm your hypothesis, and to revise your model of the world based on what you actually find rather than what you expected to find. That discipline is one I carry into every technical project I work on.

## Competency Reflections

### Learning and Reasoning

Learning and Reasoning refers to the ability to acquire new knowledge, think critically about complex problems, and apply what you have learned across different contexts. Research has been my most intensive training ground for this competency. Every project has required me to learn new material, from differential equations governing neuron behavior to the formal semantics of distributed consensus protocols, and then apply that material to produce something functional and correct.

What makes research-based learning different from coursework alone is that the answers are not in the back of the book. You have to reason from first principles, test your assumptions, and update your understanding based on results. I learned that strong reasoning is not the same as never being wrong. It is about being willing to revise. That shift in mindset has made me a better engineer and a more careful thinker. In future research and professional work, I will continue to approach learning as an active process that requires both intellectual humility and persistence.

---

### Self-Awareness and Development

Self-Awareness and Development involves recognizing where you are in your own growth, identifying gaps honestly, and taking deliberate steps to address them. One of the most formative moments in my research trajectory was recognizing that the version of computer science I had been exposed to in introductory courses was not capturing what I cared about. I had to actively seek out the intersection of computation and neuroscience, enroll in courses outside my comfort zone, and take on research roles before I fully understood what was available to me.

That experience of not fitting neatly into a single track taught me something important about self-direction. Nobody told me that computational neuroscience was a path I could pursue. I had to identify it myself and build toward it step by step. In future work, whether in graduate school or industry, I will continue to navigate my own development with that same intentionality, seeking out experiences that build toward the researcher and engineer I want to become.
