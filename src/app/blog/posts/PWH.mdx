## The Beginning

When I was younger, I came across a YouTube video about Partners for World Health (PWH), a nonprofit organization that collects and redistributes unused medical supplies to communities around the world.  

The idea stayed with me. The concept was simple but powerful: instead of allowing medical supplies to go to waste, redirect them to places where they are urgently needed.

Years later, as a student at the University of Washington, I realized something — UW did not have a chapter.

So I reached out.

I asked if I could start one here.

They said yes.

---

## Bringing Partners for World Health to UW

Founding the UW chapter required more than just approval. It meant:

- Navigating university registration processes  
- Building a founding leadership team  
- Recruiting members  
- Establishing partnerships for supply collection  
- Creating operational workflows  

As founder, I worked to translate the mission of a global nonprofit into a sustainable, student-led initiative on campus.

---

## Why It Matters

Medical supply waste is a global issue. At the same time, hospitals and clinics worldwide operate without adequate resources.

The UW chapter of Partners for World Health aims to:

- Collect unused medical supplies  
- Organize and prepare them for redistribution  
- Raise awareness about global health inequities  
- Provide students hands-on experience in public health and humanitarian work  

This work bridges engineering, healthcare, and social responsibility.

---

## Leadership & Impact

Starting the chapter required persistence and initiative.  

What began as an idea inspired by a video became a tangible organization at one of the largest public universities in the country.

Through this experience, I learned how to:

- Turn inspiration into institutional action  
- Communicate with national nonprofit leadership  
- Build systems from scratch  
- Lead with both structure and empathy  

---

## Reflection

Founding the UW chapter reinforced something I deeply believe:  

Access is not accidental. It is designed.

Whether in healthcare, education, or technology, systems either enable participation or restrict it. My goal is to build systems that expand opportunity — not limit it.

Partners for World Health at UW is one example of that commitment in action.
