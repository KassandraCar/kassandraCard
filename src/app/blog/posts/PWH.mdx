## Overview

Partners for World Health (PWH) is a nonprofit organization that collects unused and surplus medical supplies and redistributes them to communities around the world that lack adequate resources. This post documents how I founded the University of Washington chapter, what the process involved, and what I learned about building something from nothing.

---

## Context

When I was younger, I came across a YouTube video about Partners for World Health. The concept was simple but powerful: instead of allowing medical supplies to go to waste, redirect them to places where they are urgently needed. The idea stayed with me.

Years later, as a student at the University of Washington, I realized UW did not have a chapter. Medical supply waste is a real and ongoing global issue. At the same time, hospitals and clinics worldwide operate without adequate resources. That gap should not exist, and it is not inevitable. It is a structural problem that requires structural solutions.

I reached out to the national organization and asked if I could start a chapter. They said yes.

---

## My Role

As founder of the UW chapter, I was responsible for building the organization from the ground up. This meant more than just getting approval from the national organization. It required:

- Navigating the university's student organization registration process
- Recruiting and building a founding leadership team
- Establishing partnerships with medical facilities and supply donors
- Creating operational workflows for collection, sorting, and redistribution
- Developing recruitment materials and communicating the mission to prospective members

There was no existing template for this at UW. Every process had to be designed from scratch, which required persistence, flexibility, and a willingness to ask for help when I did not know how to proceed.

---

## Skills Developed

Through this experience, I developed and strengthened the following skills:

- **Organizational founding**: Building institutional structures where none existed before
- **Strategic planning**: Designing operational workflows and long-term sustainability plans
- **Cross-organizational communication**: Corresponding with national nonprofit leadership and university administration
- **Recruitment and team building**: Identifying and engaging students who shared the mission
- **Resilience**: Navigating bureaucratic processes and setbacks without losing momentum
- **Mission alignment**: Translating a global nonprofit's goals into a workable local chapter model

---

## Impact

What began as an idea inspired by a video became a tangible student organization at one of the largest public universities in the country. The UW chapter of Partners for World Health aims to:

- Collect unused medical supplies from donors across the campus and surrounding community
- Organize and prepare supplies for redistribution to under-resourced healthcare settings
- Raise awareness among students about global health inequities and medical supply waste
- Provide students with hands-on experience in public health and humanitarian work

Founding the chapter also created a new pathway for UW students to engage with global health work at the undergraduate level, something that had not previously existed through this organization on campus.

---

## Reflection

Founding the UW chapter of Partners for World Health reinforced something I believe deeply: access is not accidental. It is designed. Whether in healthcare, education, or technology, systems either enable participation or restrict it. The fact that UW had no chapter was not the result of students not caring. It was simply the result of no one yet having built the infrastructure.

This experience taught me that leadership sometimes means being the person who sees a gap and decides to fill it, not because you have all the answers, but because you are willing to figure it out along the way. Building the chapter required persistence, creativity, and a comfort with uncertainty. Those are qualities I now carry into every leadership role I take on.

## Competency Reflections

### Civic Responsibility

Civic Responsibility is about understanding and acting on our obligation to contribute to the well-being of others, especially those who face greater barriers to resources and opportunity. Founding the University of Washington chapter of Partners for World Health was one of the clearest expressions of this competency in my experience as a student leader. The organization redirects unused medical supplies to communities that urgently need them, and bringing that mission to UW meant taking an idea from inspiration to institution.

I learned that civic responsibility is not passive. It requires action: writing emails, navigating bureaucratic processes, recruiting people, and building infrastructure that can outlast any one person's involvement. I also learned that impact scales when structures are in place. One person's inspiration matters far less than the system that sustains the work beyond them. Going forward, I will continue to build with this in mind, ensuring that the initiatives I start have leadership pipelines and operational frameworks that outlast my direct involvement.

---

### Strategic Planning

Strategic Planning requires thinking beyond the immediate task and designing processes that remain functional over time. When founding the UW chapter of Partners for World Health, I had to think not just about getting the organization registered, but about building recruiting pipelines, logistics workflows, and community partnerships that would sustain the chapter after I graduated.

I created systems for supply collection and redistribution, drafted organizational documents, and coordinated with national PWH leadership to ensure the chapter aligned with the broader mission. I learned that founding something is very different from running something that already exists. Without clear systems, even well-intentioned organizations stall. This experience taught me to think structurally and plan for sustainability from the very beginning. In future work, whether in engineering or community organizing, I will apply this lesson by building with longevity in mind rather than focusing only on short-term milestones.

---

### Self-Awareness and Development

Self-Awareness and Development refers to the ability to reflect honestly on your strengths, gaps, and growth over time. Founding Partners for World Health at UW challenged me to grow quickly and in ways I had not anticipated. I had no prior experience starting an organization from nothing, and there were moments of genuine uncertainty about whether I was the right person for the role or whether the project was even feasible.

Sitting with that uncertainty and choosing to move forward anyway was itself a form of development. I learned that leadership growth rarely comes from comfort. It comes from taking on responsibilities that feel slightly beyond what you know you can do. What made the difference was not a sudden surge of confidence but a commitment to learning as I went. I will carry this forward by continuing to pursue roles and projects that stretch my capabilities, and by approaching uncertainty as a signal that growth is happening rather than as a reason to pull back.
