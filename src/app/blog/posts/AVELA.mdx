## Overview

AVELA (A Vision for Engineering Literacy and Access) is a student-led organization at the University of Washington dedicated to expanding access to engineering education and opportunity. As Co-President, I help coordinate the organization's operations, partnerships, and programming. This experience represents one of the most formative leadership roles I have held as a student, and it has shaped how I think about equity, access, and what it means to build a community with intention.

---

## Context

Access to engineering education remains deeply unequal. Many students, particularly those from under-resourced high schools or first-generation college backgrounds, lack clear information about scholarships, fellowships, and graduate pathways. They may never encounter diverse engineering role models or receive mentorship that helps them see themselves in technical careers.

AVELA was founded to address exactly these gaps. The organization operates at the intersection of education, mentorship, and access, working to reduce systemic barriers and build sustained pathways into STEM fields. When I became involved, I saw an opportunity to connect my technical background with my commitment to equity and community.

---

## My Role

As Co-President of AVELA, I share responsibility for the full scope of the organization's operations. My responsibilities include:

- Coordinating board operations and driving strategic planning each semester
- Building and maintaining institutional partnerships with university departments and external organizations
- Managing budget proposals and funding processes
- Leading event logistics for scholarship nights, speaker series, and K-12 outreach
- Supporting student leaders on the board as they develop their own leadership skills
- Representing AVELA in conversations with campus administrators and community partners

This role required me to grow quickly in areas where I had limited experience, including financial management, cross-organizational communication, and long-term planning.

---

## Skills Developed

Through my leadership at AVELA, I developed and strengthened the following skills:

- **Organizational leadership**: Managing a board of students with different responsibilities, schedules, and priorities
- **Strategic planning**: Designing semester-long programming calendars and multi-year partnership strategies
- **Public communication**: Presenting AVELA's mission to prospective members, administrators, and community partners
- **Budget management**: Overseeing funding proposals and tracking organizational expenses
- **Facilitation**: Running productive board meetings and navigating disagreements constructively
- **Community outreach**: Coordinating programs that reached K-12 students and university peers

---

## Impact

AVELA has engaged hundreds of students annually through outreach programs, speaker events, and mentorship initiatives. Specific outcomes from my time as Co-President include:

- Hosting scholarship and graduate school information nights that connected students with funding they might not have independently discovered
- Expanding outreach partnerships with high schools across Washington
- Supporting students in navigating professional and academic pathways through one-on-one and group mentorship
- Creating speaker programming that centered representation and career storytelling from engineers with diverse backgrounds

The goal has never been simply to inspire interest in engineering. It has been to ensure students have the structural support to persist and thrive once they arrive.

## Reflection

Leading AVELA has clarified something I believe deeply: access is not accidental. It is designed. The students who find their way into engineering careers are often not those with the most talent, but those who had access to information, mentorship, and community at the right time. AVELA is an attempt to interrupt that pattern.

What I have learned as Co-President is that effective leadership in this kind of organization requires you to hold both the big picture and the small details at the same time. You have to care about the mission without losing sight of the logistics. You have to be present for the people in the room while also building structures that will serve the people who are not yet there.

This experience has also challenged me to examine my own assumptions about what "access" looks like. It is not enough to create opportunities. Those opportunities have to be communicated clearly, made easy to participate in, and designed with the needs of the community in mind. That lesson will inform how I approach engineering and leadership work throughout my career.

## Competency Reflections

### Civic Responsibility

Civic Responsibility refers to the obligation individuals have to contribute to the welfare of their communities, particularly those who face systemic barriers to opportunity. As Co-President of AVELA, I demonstrated this competency by coordinating outreach programs that connected K-12 students and university peers with engineering resources, scholarships, and mentorship they would not otherwise have easily accessed. My role required me to think beyond personal or organizational goals and ask how AVELA could serve the broader community.

I learned that civic responsibility in a leadership context means being accountable not just to the people in the room, but to those who are not yet there but who deserve to be. Going forward, I plan to apply this by ensuring that the technical systems and organizations I help build include people from underrepresented backgrounds in their design process, not just as end users. Leadership that does not extend toward community is incomplete.

---

### Group Dynamics

Group Dynamics refers to the patterns of interaction, decision-making, and role distribution within a team or organization. Leading AVELA's board required me to navigate competing priorities, facilitate productive meetings, and ensure every member felt their contributions were valued. I demonstrated this competency by creating meeting structures that gave each board member space to share updates and raise concerns, and by mediating disagreements in a way that kept the group moving forward.

What I learned is that effective group dynamics do not happen automatically. They require intentional design: setting shared norms, distributing responsibility clearly, and creating psychological safety so people feel comfortable contributing honestly. In future engineering and professional leadership roles, I will bring the same attention to team culture, recognizing that diverse teams produce stronger outcomes when their dynamics are thoughtfully managed.

---

### Strategic Planning

Strategic Planning is the process of identifying long-term goals, assessing available resources and constraints, and designing actionable steps to move toward those goals. At AVELA, this meant translating our mission of expanding engineering access into semester plans, event timelines, partnership proposals, and budget projections.

I learned that strategic planning requires both clarity about what you are trying to achieve and flexibility when circumstances change. A plan that cannot adapt is not a strong plan. I also learned to prioritize initiatives based on community impact rather than convenience or ease of execution. In future leadership roles, whether in technical project management or organizational work, I will use strategic planning as a framework for keeping teams aligned, resources allocated effectively, and goals connected to a clear and meaningful purpose.

---

### Communication

Communication, in a leadership context, encompasses both how information is shared and how it is received. Serving as Co-President of AVELA required me to communicate across multiple audiences: board members, student participants, university administrators, and external partners. Each audience required a different tone and level of detail.

I learned that clear communication builds trust and reduces confusion, especially in organizations where members are volunteers balancing many other responsibilities. One specific lesson I carry forward is that overcommunicating is rarely the problem in student organizations. Under-communicating almost always is. In future professional and leadership contexts, I plan to prioritize transparent communication as a core practice, ensuring that the people I work with have the information they need to succeed and feel respected as contributors.
